Key Aspects of Aquatic and Marine Wildlife -

Habitats: Aquatic habitats include freshwater (rivers, lakes) and marine (oceans, estuaries). Marine zones are divided by light availability (photic vs. aphotic) and depth (benthic, neritic).

Biodiversity: Over 242,000 documented marine species exist, with roughly two million yet to be discovered.

Marine Mammals: Include cetaceans (whales, dolphins), pinnipeds (seals, walruses), and sea otters, which are warm-blooded and often rely on blubber for insulation.

Invertebrates: Diverse groups including mollusks (squid, octopus) and cnidarians (jellyfish, corals) that often form complex, essential ecosystems.

Adaptations: Marine life has evolved unique features for pressure, darkness, and salinity, such as streamlining for speed, gills for breathing, and, in many fish, ectothermic (cold-blooded) metabolism.

Threats and Conservation: Marine ecosystems face severe threats, including overfishing (depleting over 70% of fish stocks), pollution, and climate change. Less than 1% of marine systems have protection, making conservation crucial for protecting the species and the over 1 billion people relying on them for protein.


"Marine animals are often categorized by their position in the water column: plankton (drifters), nekton (swimmers), and benthos (bottom dwellers). "
