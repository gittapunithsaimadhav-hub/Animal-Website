Key Characteristics of Primates -

Adaptations: Features include forward-facing eyes for binocular vision, large brains, and nails instead of claws, aiding in grasping branches.

Locomotion: Many are arboreal, moving via leaping, swinging (brachiation), or quadrupedalism in the canopy.

Diet: Generally omnivorous, relying heavily on fruit, leaves, and insects.

Social Structure: They live in varied groups, from solitary individuals to complex social troops.


Forest Environments and Inhabitants -

* Primates dominate tropical forest canopies, but are one part of a complex ecosystem. Other forest animals include:

Arboreal Specialists: Sloths (3-toed), tree-shrews, and various monkeys.

Ground Dwellers: Large apes like gorillas and various terrestrial monkeys.

Camouflaged Species: The okapi (related to the giraffe) lives in dense African forests.


Primate Divisions -

Prosimians: Lemurs, lorises, and galagos.

New World Monkeys: Found in Central/South America, many have prehensile tails.

Old World Monkeys & Apes: Found in Africa and Asia.


Ecological Importance -

Primates are vital for biodiversity. By eating fruit and traveling, they disperse seeds, which helps maintain forest structure and growth.
