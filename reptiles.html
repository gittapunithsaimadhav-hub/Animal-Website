Key Characteristics of Reptiles -

Skin & Respiration: Reptiles have tough, dry, scaly skin, which allows them to live in dry habitats and away from water sources.

Temperature Regulation: They are ecotherms (cold-blooded), meaning they rely on environmental heat to regulate their body temperature, often basking to stay warm.

Reproduction: Almost all reptiles lay eggs on land (oviparous), although a few species, such as some snakes, give birth to live young (viviparous).

Habitat: While many are terrestrial, some are highly adapted for aquatic or marine environments.

Diet: Most are carnivorous or insectivorous, although some are herbivorous.


Major Groups (Orders) -

Squamata: Snakes, lizards, and worm lizards.

Testudines: Turtles and tortoises.

Crocodylia: Crocodiles, alligators, caimans, and gharials.

Rhynchocephalia: The tuatara.


Other Facts & "Others" -

Evolution: Reptiles dominated the Earth for 186 million years during the Mesozoic era.

Herpetology: The study of reptiles is combined with the study of amphibians, known as herpetology.

Dietary Habits: Many reptiles, particularly crocodiles, swallow their food whole.

Conservation: Many species are threatened by habitat loss, pollution, and overexploitation.

Health Warning: Pet reptiles and amphibians can carry Salmonella bacteria, making proper handling and hygiene essential.
